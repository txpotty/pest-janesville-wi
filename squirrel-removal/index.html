Our Detailed Squirrel Removal Process

Initial Inspection and Assessment

The first critical step in our squirrel removal service is a thorough inspection of your property. Our experienced technicians will carefully examine both the interior and exterior of your home or business to identify all potential and active squirrel entry points. This includes areas such as roofs, attics, chimneys, vents, windows, and any gaps or cracks in the structure. Developing a Customized Removal Plan

Based on the findings from our initial inspection, we will develop a customized squirrel removal plan designed to address your specific needs and the unique characteristics of your property. Our goal is to provide you with a clear understanding of the process and set realistic expectations for the outcome. Signs You Might Need Squirrel Removal Services in Janesville

Squirrels, despite their seemingly harmless appearance, can be incredibly destructive and persistent when seeking shelter and nesting sites within human structures. Recognizing the early signs of a squirrel infestation is crucial for minimizing damage and addressing the problem before it escalates. Ignoring these indicators can lead to significant structural issues, potential fire hazards due to chewed electrical wiring, and unsanitary conditions. Here are some common signs that suggest you may need professional squirrel removal services in Janesville, Wisconsin:

Unusual Noises Coming From the Attic or Walls

One of the most common indicators of a squirrel presence inside your home is the sound of scratching, scampering, or thumping coming from your attic, walls, or even crawl spaces. Squirrels are most active during the day, particularly in the morning and late afternoon. These noises are often the result of them moving around, building nests, or storing food. If you hear persistent noises that seem to originate from within your building's structure, especially during daylight hours, it's a strong possibility that squirrels have taken up residence. These sounds can range from light skittering to more pronounced gnawing, depending on the squirrel's activity. Differentiating between squirrel noises and those of other pests like mice or rats can sometimes be tricky, but squirrels are generally larger and their movements might sound heavier. Don't dismiss these sounds; they are your first warning sign.

The noises you hear can provide clues about the squirrels' activities. Scratching might indicate gnawing on wood or other materials, while scurrying suggests they are exploring or moving between locations. Understanding these sounds helps our technicians pinpoint potential entry points and nesting areas during the inspection. It's essential to pay close attention to the timing and location of these sounds as they provide valuable information for diagnosis. Squirrels are also known to make chattering or barking sounds, particularly when agitated or communicating with each other outside the property. While these vocalizations don't necessarily mean they are inside, increased vocal activity around your home could be a precursor to an indoor infestation as they scout for potential entry points and suitable nesting locations.

Visible Gnaw Marks and Damage

Squirrels have a constant need to gnaw to keep their teeth from growing too long. This natural behavior can lead to significant damage when they gain access to your property. You may notice visible gnaw marks on wood, insulation, wiring, pipes, and other materials in your attic, walls, or around entry points. Chewed electrical wires are a particularly serious concern as they can create a significant fire hazard. Squirrels can also damage rafters, beams, and other structural components, compromising the integrity of your building. Looking for these signs during routine inspections of your attic or crawl space is crucial. Even small signs of gnawing should be investigated further. They are relentless chewers and can cause extensive damage in a relatively short period. This damage can be costly to repair if not addressed promptly.

Beyond structural elements and wiring, squirrels may also chew on plumbing pipes, potentially leading to leaks and water damage. They can also damage personal belongings stored in attics or garages. The discovery of wood shavings, frayed wires, or damaged insulation is a clear indication of squirrel activity. These signs are often most evident near potential entry points such as vents, gables, or chimneys. Our technicians are experienced in identifying and assessing the extent of this damage during our inspections. Documenting the damage is also helpful for insurance purposes if a claim needs to be filed for repairs. Recognizing and acting quickly on these signs can save you considerable expense and frustration down the line.

Presence of Droppings and Urine Stains

Like any animal, squirrels will leave behind waste, including droppings and urine. The presence of squirrel droppings in your attic, garage, or other areas signifies an active infestation. Squirrel droppings are typically small, pellet-shaped, and may resemble large rice grains. The accumulation of droppings and urine can create an unsanitary environment, produce foul odors, and potentially pose health risks. Urine stains on ceilings or walls may also be indicative of squirrels nesting or traveling within those spaces. These stains can appear yellowish or brownish and may be accompanied by a strong, musky odor. Identifying these signs early can give you a clear indication of the severity and location of the infestation.

Squirrel urine can also soak into insulation and wood, causing staining and deterioration of materials. The odor of squirrel urine can be persistent and difficult to remove without professional cleaning. Areas with heavy squirrel activity will often have a concentrated presence of droppings. It's important to exercise caution when cleaning up animal waste, as it can contain bacteria or parasites. Leaving this task to professionals who have the proper protective equipment and cleaning solutions is recommended. During our inspection, we will assess the extent of fecal and urine contamination and discuss remediation options if necessary. The location and amount of waste can also help us determine how long the squirrels have been present and the primary areas of their activity within your property structure.

Visual Sightings of Squirrels Entering or Exiting the Building

While noises and droppings provide strong evidence of an infestation, actually seeing squirrels entering or exiting your building is a definitive confirmation. Observe your home or business during the day, particularly in the morning and late afternoon, to see if you notice squirrels repeatedly using a specific entry point. These entry points can be located on the roof, under eaves, through damaged vents, or even gaps in the siding. Squirrels are remarkably agile and can climb walls and navigate challenging structures with ease. Seeing them carrying nesting materials, such as leaves or twigs, into your building is a clear sign that they are establishing a den. Their behavior can also indicate whether the infestation is a single squirrel or a family.

Pay attention to where the squirrels are coming and going. This information is extremely valuable for our technicians during the inspection phase, as it helps us quickly locate the main points of entry and focus our removal efforts accordingly. Sometimes, squirrels may be seen peeking out of holes or gaps in the structure. Their consistent presence around your property, especially close to the building, should raise a red flag and warrant investigation. Don't underestimate their ability to find even the smallest openings. Even if you only see one squirrel, they may be part of a larger family unit or actively seeking to establish a new territory within your home. Taking prompt action upon visual confirmation is crucial to prevent the infestation from growing.

Increased Squirrel Activity on Your Property

A general increase in the number of squirrels seen on your property, particularly close to your home or business, can be another indicator of an impending or existing problem. If you suddenly notice more squirrels in your yard, on your roof, or around your foundation, they may be exploring potential entry points or be attracted to something on your property, such as food sources or nesting materials. While a few squirrels in your yard are normal, an unusually high concentration warrants closer examination. This increased activity might also involve aggressive behavior towards other squirrels as they establish territory.

Ant control and other general pest services are different from wildlife control, and a surge in squirrel activity is a specific sign to look for. This heightened presence often indicates they are actively seeking shelter and could be attempting to find ways into your building as the weather changes or if they are displaced from their natural habitat. Observing their behavior, such as repeatedly climbing walls or running along power lines towards your structure, can provide valuable clues. They might also be seen gathering nesting materials like twigs, leaves, or even insulation fibers from around your property. Don't dismiss an unusual surge in squirrel sightings; it's often a preliminary sign of potential trouble. Consider if there are attractants on your property, but regardless, increased activity should prompt further investigation for potential entry points.

Damaged Attic Insulation

Squirrels frequently use attic insulation as nesting material, shredding and moving it to create a comfortable den. If you inspect your attic and find that your insulation is disturbed, matted down, or has visible tunnels or nests within it, it's a strong sign of squirrel activity. This can significantly reduce the effectiveness of your insulation, leading to higher energy bills. The damage isn't just cosmetic; damaged insulation can harbor droppings and urine, creating a health hazard and foul odors. Squirrels are very adept at maneuvering through insulation, creating pathways and nesting pockets that are often hidden from view. Identifying this damage requires a physical inspection of your attic space. Look for areas where the insulation appears flattened, pulled apart, or where you can clearly see nested material within it.

The type of insulation doesn't deter them; they will readily damage fiberglass, cellulose, or other common types. Beyond nesting, squirrels can also damage the vapor barrier and other layers of your attic's structure. The presence of ripped or torn insulation is a strong visual cue. Our technicians are experienced in identifying this type of damage and can accurately assess the extent of the squirrels' presence within your attic based on the condition of the insulation. Addressing damaged insulation is not only important for pest control but also for maintaining the energy efficiency and integrity of your home's thermal envelope. Professional cleanup and remediation of contaminated insulation is also often recommended to ensure a safe and healthy environment after the squirrels are removed from inside. Consider combining squirrel removal with an attic cleanup service for a complete solution.

Foul Odors Present

A persistent, unpleasant odor in your home or business, particularly in areas like the attic, walls, or near vents, can be another indicator of a squirrel infestation. These odors are often caused by the accumulation of squirrel droppings and urine, as well as the decaying remains of nesting materials or even deceased squirrels. Squirrel urine has a strong, musky smell that can be quite pungent, especially in enclosed spaces. As the infestation grows, the odor can become more pronounced and seep into other areas of your building. Ignoring these smells is not advisable, as they are not only unpleasant but can also signal potential health hazards. The odor might be strongest in warmer weather or in areas with poor ventilation, as the heat can intensify the smell of urine and decomposition. Pinpointing the source of the odor can help identify the infected areas.

The presence of a sweet, sickly smell might indicate a deceased squirrel within the walls or attic, which requires immediate attention. Foul odors can also attract other pests, compounding the problem. Identifying the specific type of odor can sometimes provide clues about the nature of the infestation. For example, a strong ammonia-like smell is often indicative of rodent urine. Our technicians are trained to identify and assess these odors during our inspections. Addressing the source of the odor is essential for a complete squirrel removal service, and this may involve cleaning and disinfecting affected areas. Simply masking the smell will not solve the underlying problem. Rodent control in general involves similar odor issues, highlighting the importance of professional intervention.

Evidence of Nests

Squirrels build nests, often referred to as dreys, using materials like leaves, twigs, insulation, and shredded fabric. These nests can be found in attics, walls, chimneys, or even inside structural voids. Discovering a squirrel's nest on your property, particularly inside your home or business, is a clear indication of an active infestation. These nests are typically messy and can accumulate droppings, urine, and discarded food debris, creating unsanitary conditions. Identifying a nest requires a thorough search of potential nesting sites. Our technicians are skilled in locating these hidden nests during our inspections. The size and complexity of the nest can offer clues about how long the squirrels have been present and whether it's a breeding nest. Female squirrels often build larger, more substantial nests when they are preparing to give birth.

Removing squirrel nests requires caution, as the nesting materials can contain parasites like fleas or mites. It's important to avoid direct contact with nesting materials and utilize proper protective gear. Once a nest is identified and removed, it's crucial to clean and disinfect the area thoroughly. Failing to remove the nest or properly clean the area can leave behind residual odors and attract other pests. The presence of nesting materials outside of the typical backyard tree can be a significant indicator that squirrels are attempting to find shelter within your structure. Look for accumulations of leaves, twigs, or shredded material in unusual places around your home's exterior, as this could be a preparatory step before they find an entry point inside. Wildlife control often involves identifying and safely removing animal nests.

Increased Electric Bills

While not as direct a sign as noises or droppings, a sudden and unexplained increase in your electric bill could be linked to a squirrel infestation. As mentioned earlier, squirrels are notorious for chewing on electrical wiring. Damaged or exposed wires can lead to short circuits, power outages, and increased electricity consumption. If your electric bill has spiked without a corresponding increase in usage, it's worth considering the possibility of a squirrel infestation and the potential damage they have caused to your electrical system. This is a less obvious sign, but it can be a significant indicator, especially when combined with other subtle clues of squirrel activity. The damage to wiring can be internal and not immediately visible, making this sign particularly concerning.

Chewing on electrical wires can also be a significant fire hazard, making prompt action essential. The insulation on wires provides an attractive material for squirrels to gnaw on. If you suspect squirrels have damaged your electrical system, it's crucial to contact both a pest control professional and an electrician to assess and repair the damage. Ignoring this potential issue could have serious consequences. The increased electrical load caused by damaged wiring or short circuits can manifest as higher bills. Even if the damage is minor, over time it can contribute to energy loss and increased costs. Consider this possibility if you've exhausted other explanations for a sudden jump in your electricity expenses. Residential and commercial properties are both susceptible to this issue.

Damage to Exterior of Building

Squirrels may also target weaker areas of the structure, such as rotting wood or poorly sealed points. Identifying and repairing these exterior vulnerabilities is essential for preventing entry in the first place. Squirrels will often exploit existing weaknesses in the building envelope. For example, damaged soffits or fascia boards provide easy access points. They are persistent when trying to find a way in, and even small gaps can be widened through persistent gnawing. Inspecting your roof and eaves regularly, especially after storms or periods of heavy wind, can help you identify potential entry points before they become major problems.

Chimneys are also common entry points, particularly uncapped ones. Squirrels can climb down chimneys seeking shelter. Damaged or missing chimney caps should be replaced or repaired promptly. Vents are another vulnerable area, as squirrels can chew through plastic or metal screening. Securing vents with sturdy, squirrel-proof mesh is a recommended preventative measure. The key is to make your building's exterior as resistant as possible to squirrel intrusion. Our technicians perform a thorough exterior inspection as part of our service to identify all potential entry points and recommend necessary repairs or preventative measures. Addressing these exterior vulnerabilities is a proactive step towards keeping squirrels out. They are opportunistic and will take advantage of any weakness they find. Consider having your roof and exterior regularly inspected to identify and seal potential entry points before they become problems. Protecting your home's exterior is the first line of defense against squirrels.
